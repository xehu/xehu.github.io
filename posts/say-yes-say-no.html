Say Yes; Say No.

  • April 1, 2020
  • By Xinlan Emily Hu

Last night, I applied to graduate from Stanford. In my "Stanford exit survey" (a Qualtrics feedback survey we're all required to fill out before we're able to graduate), I was asked to give a piece of advice to future Stanford students.

I paused, my mind awash with memory. One piece of advice.

Ultimately, if Stanford has taught me one thing, it is this: learn how to say yes, and then how to say no.

Say yes to the opportunity that excites you, but also scares you a little bit. Take the leap -- take a class in a field you haven't explored; try to fly untethered by worries and self-doubt. Say yes to trying a new club, even to just *trying out* for a club (I once auditioned for an a cappella group, despite my inability to sing), to new experiences. I, a person with two left feet, decided to take social dancing at Stanford on a whim, and ended up as the president of Stanford Viennese Ball. I took a research opportunity, and ended up writing an Honors Thesis. While abroad, I joined my friends on a last-minute trip to Amsterdam. I loved it. SAY YES.

But when the opportunities bombard you, when you feel like you're pulled in a million directions, learn to say no. Say no when you realize that something is hurting your mental health. There is no shame in quitting, no loss in saying goodbye to something you once invested heavily in. You've already gained the valuable skill of willpower---the very power make such difficult decisions. Say no when you realize that you've got a path all your own; when you compare yourself too much to every other high-achieving person here, and you want to enroll in ALL the units and do ALL the clubs and apply for ALL the jobs and ALL the fellowships. In those moments, SAY NO.
